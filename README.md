## Why This Tool Exists — The Academic Case

Before a single pixel is designed, the research must be understood. This tool is not a quiz. It is a psychometric instrument disguised as entertainment. Every design decision traces back to one of the following bodies of research.

**Behavioural Economics Foundation**

Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory (1979) established that humans do not evaluate financial outcomes rationally. We evaluate them relative to a reference point, and losses feel approximately 2.3 times more painful than equivalent gains feel good. This asymmetry explains why most people avoid checking their bank accounts when they suspect bad news — the anticipated pain of the number is worse than the uncertainty. The tool must design around this. Questions cannot feel like an audit. The framing must be curious and warm, not evaluative.

Thaler's Mental Accounting research (1985, 1999) showed that people treat money differently depending on its perceived source and intended purpose. A tax refund feels like free money and gets spent. A salary feels earned and gets protected. A windfall from gambling feels different from a bonus at work. This is irrational by classical economics but completely predictable. The tool uses this to generate surprising insights — showing users the invisible mental accounts they are already running without knowing it.

Shefrin and Thaler's Self-Control research (1988) identified the planner-doer conflict in financial decision-making. The planner knows what to do. The doer does something else. The gap between knowing and doing is the single most common financial experience in the 18 to 30 cohort. The tool's most important job is to make users feel seen about this gap, not ashamed of it.

Ariely's work on irrational decision-making — particularly his research on arbitrary anchoring and the price of zero — informs how the waitlist CTA is designed. Free things feel categorically different from cheap things. The waitlist must feel like zero friction, zero cost, zero risk.

**Psychological Foundation**

Klontz, Britt, Mentzer and Klontz (2011) developed the Money Script Inventory through empirical research and identified four core belief patterns that drive financial behaviour across cultures: Money Avoidance, Money Worship, Money Status, and Money Vigilance. Their research shows these scripts are largely inherited from family of origin, operate below conscious awareness, and are strong predictors of financial outcomes including net worth, income, and financial distress. This is the backbone of Question 4 in the tool.

Furnham's work on the psychology of money (1984 onwards) demonstrated that money attitudes are stable personality traits rather than situational responses. This validates the entire premise of the personality test — your relationship with money is genuinely consistent and predictable, which means a small number of well-chosen questions can reveal something true and surprising.

Griskevicius, Tybur and Van den Bergh (2010) showed that early life economic environments leave lasting traces on adult financial behaviour — people who grew up in environments of financial scarcity are measurably more present-biased, more risk-averse, and more susceptible to immediate temptations even after their financial situation improves. This gives the tool cultural sensitivity depth — the insights generated need to land differently for different life histories.

**HCI and UX Foundation**

Fogg's Behaviour Model (2009) defines behaviour as the intersection of Motivation, Ability, and Prompt. For the tool to convert to waitlist signups, all three must be present at the moment of the CTA. Motivation is built through the story cards — the user feels seen and curious. Ability is maximised by making the signup a single email field with no password, no verification friction. Prompt is the final story card's CTA, timed at peak emotional engagement.

Norman's principles from The Design of Everyday Things inform every interaction pattern. Affordances must be obvious. Feedback must be immediate. Errors must be recoverable. The user must always feel in control, particularly around sensitive financial questions.

Csikszentmihalyi's Flow theory informs the pacing of the question sequence. Flow requires a balance between challenge and ability. Questions that are too easy feel trivial. Questions that are too hard create anxiety. Each question is calibrated to feel just slightly more introspective than the previous one, creating a gentle escalating depth that pulls the user forward.

Self-Determination Theory (Ryan and Deci, 2000) identifies autonomy, competence, and relatedness as the three core psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation. The tool satisfies all three — autonomy through framed choices rather than open-ended inputs, competence through personality results that validate their self-knowledge, relatedness through comparison data that contextualises them within a human community.

**Humour and Shareability Research**

McGraw and Warren's Benign Violation Theory (2010) provides the academic framework for why the meme card works. Something is funny when it is simultaneously a violation of expectation and benign — not threatening. "You spent $1,200 on food delivery and called it cooking at home" is a violation of self-perception that is benign because there is no real harm. This is the precise register the meme card must hit. Violating but safe.

Berger and Milkman's research on viral content (2012) identified the two strongest predictors of shareability: practical value and social currency. Content gets shared when it is useful or when sharing it makes the sharer look good or interesting. The personality result gives social currency — it says something true and interesting about the sharer. The number card gives practical value. Both must be present.
